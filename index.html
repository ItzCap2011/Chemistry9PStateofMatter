States of Matter

Explore the 4 states, how particles behave, and why matter changes state!

🧊
SOLID
  • Fixed shape & fixed volume
  • Very low compressibility
  • Particles vibrate in fixed positions
  • Lowest energy state
  • Strong intermolecular forces
🧊 Ice🪨 Rock 🥄 Metal spoon🧱 Brick
📏 Particle distance: ~0.3 nm — particles are packed very closely together in a regular lattice. Almost no space between them!
💪 Force strength: Very strong intermolecular forces hold particles tightly in fixed positions. Hard to compress.
🔥 Kinetic Energy (KE): LOWEST — particles only vibrate, no movement through space.
🔗 Potential Energy (PE): HIGHEST — maximum energy stored in the strong bonds between particles.
💧
LIQUID
  • Takes shape of container
  • Fixed volume
  • Low compressibility
  • Particles slide past each other
  • More energy than solids
💧 Water🛢️ Oil 🧃 Juice⚗️ Mercury
📏 Particle distance: ~0.3–0.4 nm — still very close together, similar to solids. Particles touch but can move around each other.
💪 Force strength: Moderate forces — strong enough to keep a fixed volume but weak enough to allow flow and change shape.
🔥 Kinetic Energy (KE): MEDIUM — particles slide and flow past each other, more motion than solid.
🔗 Potential Energy (PE): MEDIUM — some bonds broken compared to solid (absorbed latent heat of fusion).
💨
GAS
  • No fixed shape or volume
  • Highly compressible
  • Particles move rapidly & randomly
  • High energy state
  • Fills any container
🌬️ Air♨️ Steam O₂ OxygenCO₂
📏 Particle distance: ~3–10 nm — particles are far apart, roughly 10× the distance of solids. Huge empty spaces between particles!
💪 Force strength: Negligible — almost zero attractive force. Particles move independently and are easy to compress.
🔥 Kinetic Energy (KE): HIGHEST (of common states) — particles zip around rapidly in all directions.
🔗 Potential Energy (PE): LOW — nearly all bonds broken. Most stored PE was converted to KE during boiling.
⚡
PLASMA
  • Super high energy – highest of all!
  • Ionised gas – electrons stripped off
  • Conducts electricity
  • Found naturally & artificially
  • Most common state in the universe!
☀️ Sun⚡ Lightning 🌌 Aurora💡 Fluorescent 🔮 Plasma ball
📏 Particle distance: Very large — free ions and electrons are spread widely. No fixed structure at all.
💪 Force strength: Electromagnetic — positive ions attract free electrons across large distances. Different to normal intermolecular forces!
🔥 Kinetic Energy (KE): EXTREME — particles move at millions of m/s. Temperatures can exceed millions of °C.
🔗 Potential Energy (PE): EXTREME — electrons are fully stripped from atoms, storing enormous electromagnetic PE.

🌡️ Water State Changes
Melting & Freezing: 0°C
Boiling & Condensation: 100°C

Same temp because the process is reversible!
🔥 Why temperature STOPS during state change?
Energy breaks intermolecular forces instead of heating. This hidden energy = Latent Heat. Temp only rises again after bonds break!
⬆️ Speed Up State Changes
• Increase temperature
• Increase surface area
• Increase airflow
• Reduce pressure (for boiling)
• Stirring the liquid
🧪 Compress Gas but NOT Liquid?
Gas = huge gaps → easy to squash.
Liquid = particles already touching → nearly impossible.

Smaller container → higher gas pressure (Boyle's Law)

🌡️ Temperature–Time Heating Curve

What happens to temperature as you heat water continuously from −20°C (solid ice) all the way to steam above 100°C?

① Solid Heating
KE rises → temperature increases. Particles vibrate faster but stay locked in fixed lattice positions.
② Melting Plateau (0°C)
Temperature stays flat! Energy goes into Latent Heat of Fusion to break bonds. KE constant, PE rises.
③ Liquid Heating
KE rises again → temperature increases. Particles slide past each other with increasing speed.
④ Boiling Plateau (100°C)
Temperature stays flat again! Energy = Latent Heat of Vaporisation. This plateau is much longer than melting.
⑤ Gas Heating
KE rises rapidly → temperature increases fast. Particles now move freely with almost no intermolecular forces.
💡 The Golden Rule
Flat section = state change → PE rising, KE constant, temp stays fixed.
Slope section = heating → KE rising, PE constant, temp increases.

🧊 Solid

📏 Distance: ~0.3 nm — very close, touching
💪 Forces: Very strong attractive bonds
🔄 Arrangement: Regular crystalline lattice
⚡ Motion: Vibrate only — no translation

💧 Liquid

📏 Distance: ~0.3–0.4 nm — still close
💪 Forces: Moderate — can break & reform
🔄 Arrangement: Random, no fixed positions
⚡ Motion: Slide past each other freely

💨 Gas

📏 Distance: ~3–10 nm — very far apart
💪 Forces: Negligible — almost zero
🔄 Arrangement: Completely random, fill space
⚡ Motion: Rapid random motion in all directions

⚡ Plasma

📏 Distance: Very large (ionised gas)
💪 Forces: Electromagnetic — ions attract electrons
🔄 Arrangement: Free positive ions + free electrons
⚡ Motion: Extreme — millions of degrees

⚡ Intermolecular Force vs Distance

🔴 Repulsion (very close)
When particles are squeezed together, electron clouds overlap and push back with a very strong force — this is why solids and liquids are almost incompressible.
🔵 Attraction (medium range)
Van der Waals & intermolecular attractions pull particles together. This force is strongest in solids, weakest in gas and zero at the equilibrium separation distance.
🔥 Kinetic Energy (KE)
Energy of movement. As temperature rises, particles move faster → KE increases.

KE ∝ Temperature
More KE = higher temperature.
🔗 Potential Energy (PE)
Energy stored in bonds between particles. Increases when bonds break during a state change — this is Latent Heat!

PE increases at melting & boiling points.
Kinetic Energy (KE) — temperature
Potential Energy (PE) — bond energy
Latent heat plateau
💡 The Golden Rule:
🌡️ Temperature change: KE increases → particles speed up → temperature rises.
❄️ State change (plateau): KE stays constant → PE increases → bonds break → temperature stays flat.
📊 This is exactly why the heating curve has flat sections at 0°C and 100°C!
🔥 What is Enthalpy Change (ΔH)?
ΔH measures heat energy transferred during a process at constant pressure.
ΔH > 0 (positive) = Endothermic — energy absorbed from surroundings (feels cold).
ΔH < 0 (negative) = Exothermic — energy released to surroundings (feels warm).

📐 Boyle's Law — What is it?
At constant temperature, pressure and volume are inversely proportional:
P × V = k
where k is a constant. Each coloured curve on the graph is one isotherm (one temperature).
📦 What does Volume affect?
Decrease V → particles hit the walls more often → P increases
Increase V → particles spread out → P decreases
This is why pumping a tyre (smaller volume) increases pressure.
🌡️ What does Temperature affect?
Higher T → particles move faster → hit walls harder & more often → higher pressure at every volume.
Move the slider up — the dot shifts to a higher isotherm curve.
🔢 Reading the graph
Each hyperbolic curve = one temperature. Higher curves = hotter gas.
Hover anywhere on the graph — the probe shows P, V, and PV = k values live.
Notice PV stays nearly constant along each curve.
🧪 Real-world examples
• Syringe: push plunger in (↓V) → pressure rises → gas resists
• Scuba tank: small volume, very high pressure stores lots of gas
• Balloon at altitude: lower external pressure → balloon expands (↑V, ↓P)
⚠️ Assumptions
Boyle's Law applies to an ideal gas — where particles have no volume and no forces between them. Real gases deviate at:
• Very high pressures (particles too close)
• Very low temperatures (forces become significant)
